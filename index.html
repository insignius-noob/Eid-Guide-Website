Welcome To Your Eid Planning Guide

Eid is a significant celebration in the Islamic calendar, marking the end of Ramadan, the holy month of fasting. There are two major Eids celebrated by Muslims: Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha.

Eid Al Fitr

  • Timing: Celebrated at the end of Ramadan, the month of fasting.
  • Significance: It marks the conclusion of a month of fasting and prayer, expressing gratitude to Allah for strength to complete it.

Traditions:

  • Prayer: Special communal prayers are held in mosques or open areas.
  • Charity: Zakat al-Fitr, a form of charity, is given to those in need before the Eid prayer.
  • Feasting: Families gather to share meals, often including sweets and traditional dishes.
  • New Clothes: It's common to wear new or festive clothing.

Eid Al Adha

  • Timing: Occurs about 70 days after Eid al-Fitr, during the Islamic month of Dhu al-Hijjah, coinciding with the Hajj pilgrimage.
  • Significance: Commemorates the willingness of Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) to sacrifice his son as an act of obedience to God. It also celebrates the completion of Hajj.
  • Religious Observance: The holiday emphasizes the values of sacrifice, charity, and gratitude. It reminds Muslims of their obligation to help those in need.

Traditions:

  • Similar traditions of Eid Al Fitr are carried out along with some additionals.
  • Sacrifice (Qurbani): Families who can afford it will sacrifice an animal (commonly sheep, goats, cows, or camels) as a symbolic act of devotion. The meat is divided into three parts: one third is given to the needy, another third is shared with relatives and friends, and the remaining third is kept for the family.
  • Feasting: After the sacrifice, families gather to share meals that often include the meat from the sacrifice, along with traditional dishes and sweets.
  • Charity: Emphasis on helping those in need is particularly strong during Eid al-Adha, with many Muslims giving to charity (Zakat) before the holiday.
